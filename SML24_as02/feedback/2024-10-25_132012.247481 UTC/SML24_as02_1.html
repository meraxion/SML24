The financial services department of an insurance company receives numerous phone calls each day from people who want to make a claim against their policy. Most claims are genuine, however about 1 out of every 6 are thought to be fraudulent. To tackle this problem the company has installed a trial version of a software voice-analysis system that monitors each conversation and gives a numerical score $z$ between 0 and 1, depending on allegedly suspicious vocal intonations of the customer. Unfortunately, nobody seems to know anymore how to interpret the score in this particular version of the system...

Tests revealed that the conditional probability density of $z$, given that a claim was valid ($c = 1$) or false ($c = 0$) are \begin{eqnarray*} p(z|c=0) & = & \alpha_0 (1 - z^2) \:,\\ p(z|c=1) & = & \alpha_1 z (z + 1) \:. \end{eqnarray*}

Part 1¶

Derive the normalization constants $\alpha_0$ and $\alpha_1$.

Student's answer Score: 2.0 / 2.0 (Top)

To derive these normalization constants, we make use of the fact that these probability distributions must integrate to 1.

For $p(z \mid c = 0)$:

\begin{align} \int_0^1 \alpha_0(1-z^2) dz &= 1 \\ \int_0^1 \alpha_0 - \alpha_0 z^2 dz &= 1 \\ \left[ \alpha_0 z - \frac{1}{3} \alpha_0 2^3 \right]_0^1 &= 1 \\ \left[ \alpha_0 - \frac{1}{3} \alpha_0 \right]_{z=1} - \left[ 0-0 \right]_{z=0} +C-C &= 1 \\ \frac{2}{3} \alpha_0 &= 1 \\ \alpha_0 &= \frac{3}{2} \end{align}

For $p(z \mid c = 1)$:

\begin{align} \int_0^1 \alpha_1z(z+1) dz &= 1 \\ \int_0^1 \alpha_1z^2 + \alpha_1z dz &= 1 \\ \left[ \frac{1}{3}\alpha_1 z^3 + \frac{1}{2}\alpha_1 z^2 \right]_0^1 &= 1 \\ \left[ \frac{1}{3} \alpha_1 + \frac{1}{2} \alpha_1 \right]_{z=1} - \left[ 0-0 \right]_{z=0} +C-C&= 1 \\ \frac{5}{6} \alpha_1 &= 1 \\ \alpha_1 &= \frac{6}{5} \end{align}

First, $p(c = 0 \mid z)$:

\begin{align} p(c = 0 \mid z) &= \frac{p(z \mid c = 0)p(c=0)}{p(z)} \\ &= \frac{\frac{3}{2}(1-z^2)\frac{1}{6}}{p(z)} \\ &= \frac{\frac{3}{12}(1-z^2)}{\frac{(3z+1)(z+1)}{4}} \\ &= \frac{1-z^2}{(3z+1)(z+1)} \\ &= \frac{-(z^2 -1)}{(3z+1)(z+1)} \\ &= \frac{-(z-1)(z+1)}{(3z+1)(z+1)} \\ &= \frac{-(z-1)}{3z+1} \\ &= \frac{-z+1}{3z+1} \end{align}

Compute the optimal decision boundary (based on our numerical score $z$) that minimizes the misclassification rate. For which $z$ should we classify $c = 0$ (false) and for which $z$ should we classify $c = 1$ (valid)? Explain your decision.

Student's answer Score: 5.0 / 6.0 (Top)

In this case, we should simply classify each data point (insurance claim) to the class that has the highest posterior probability given the test score. We see that if the test score is below 0.2, $p(c=0 \mid z)$ has higher probability, and if it's above $0.2$, $p(c=1\mid z)$ has the higher posterior probability. Therefore, we would accept any claims with $z \geq 0.2$, and otherwise reject.

We can land on this decision boundary in another way through looking at the probability of being correct:

\begin{align} p(\text{correct}) &= \sum_{k=1}^K p(z \in \mathcal{R}_k,c_k) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^K \int_{\mathcal{R}_k} p(z, c_k) \mathrm{d}z \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^K \int_{\mathcal{R}_k} p(c_k \mid z) p(z) \mathrm{d}z \end{align} We want to maximise this function, and through reasoning, we can see that it's maximised when we choose $z$ to be in the class that maximises the conditional probability $p(C_k \mid z)$, because $p(z)$ is in each term of the sums.

In graphical terms, we look at the plots for the two conditional (posterior) distributions $p(C_k \mid z), k=1,2$, and see where they cross.

One more way to phrase this: We can also see that when $p(C_1 \mid z) > p(C_2 \mid z)$, we assign the case to class 1, and vice versa, and note that the value for $z$ where that inequality flips is the optimal decision boundary (between those two classes).

Part 5¶

Compute the misclassification rate, given the optimal decision boundary determined previously. Interpret the result you have obtained. Is the $z$ score useful in determining the validity of the claim?

Student's answer Score: 1.0 / 6.0 (Top)

Here, we want to compute the $p(\text{mistake})$. This can be computed by:

\begin{align} p(\text{mistake}) &= \int_{R_0} p(z, c=1) dz + \int_{R_1} p(z, c=0) dz \\ &= \int_0^{0.2} p(z, c=1) dz + \int_{0.2}^1 p(z, c=0) dz \\ &= \int_0^{0.2} p(c=1 \mid z) p(c=1) dz + \int_{0.2}^1 p(c=0 \mid z)p(c=0) dz \end{align}

We can see this naturally aligns with the (two) ways of making a mistake in this case; we can either get a false positive (thinking that a claim is false when it is actually true), or a false negative (thinking that a claim is true when it is actually false). Because we decide that a claim is false when $z <0.2$, that's the decision boundary in our case and therefore where our integrands end/start.

Let's get our distributions straight before we begin. We have four of them to care about: \begin{align} p(c=1 \mid z) &= \frac{4z}{3z+1}\\ p(c=1) &= 5/6 \\ p(c=0 \mid z) &= \frac{-z+1}{3z+1}\\ p(c=0) &= 1/6 \end{align}

Let's deal with each of these integrals in turn: \begin{align} \int_0^{0.2} \frac{4z}{3z+1}\frac{5}{6} dz &= \int_0^{0.2} \frac{20z}{6(3z+1)} dz \\ &= \int_0^{0.2} \frac{10z}{3(3z+1)} dz \\ &= \frac{10}{3} \int_0^{0.2} \frac{z}{3z+1} dz \\ &= \frac{10}{3} \int_0^{0.2} \frac{u-1}{3u}\frac{du}{3dz}dz \\ &= \frac{10}{27} \int_0^{0.2} \frac{u-1}{u} du \\ &= \frac{10}{27} \int_0^{0.2} \frac{u}{u} - \frac{1}{u} du \\ &= +frac{10}{27} \int_0^{0.2} 1 - \frac{1}{u} du \\ &= \left[ \frac{10}{27}(u - \ln(u)) \right]_0^{0.2} \\ &= \left[ \frac{10}{27} (3z+1 - \ln(3z+1)) \right]_0^{0.2} \end{align} Note that in step 4, I substitute in $u = 3z+1$. For the denominator, this is trivial, but for the numerator, $z \rightarrow \frac{3z}{3} = \frac{3z+1-1}{3} = \frac{u-1}{3}$.

Now, this we can evaluate at 0.2 and 0 to solve the definite integral: \begin{align} &= \left[ \frac{10}{27} (3(0.2)+1 - \ln(3(0.2)+1)) \right] - \left[ \frac{10}{27} (3(0)+1 - \ln(3(0)+1)) \right] \\ &= 0.04814680398 \end{align}

That's the first integral done. Now moving on to the second:

\begin{align} \int_{0.2}^1 p(c=0 \mid z) p(c=0)dz &= \int_{0.2}^1 \frac{-z+1}{3z+1} \frac{1}{6} dz \\ &= \frac{1}{6} \int_{0.2}^1 \frac{u-4}{3u} \frac{du}{3dz}dz \\ &= \frac{1}{6} \frac{1}{9} \int_{0.2}^1 \frac{u-4}{u} du \\ &= \frac{1}{54} \int_{0.2}^1 \frac{u}{u} - \frac{4}{u} du \\ &= \frac{1}{54} \int_{0.2}^1 1 - 4 \frac{1}{u} du \\ &= \frac{1}{54} \int_{0.2}^1 1 \;du - 4 \int_{0.2}^1\frac{1}{u} du \\ &= \left [ \frac{1}{54}(u - 4 \ln u) \right]_{0.2}^1 \\ &= \left [ \frac{1}{54} (3z+1 - 4 \ln 3z+1) \right]_{0.2}^1 \end{align}

This we can once again evaluate at 1 and 0.2 to solve the definite integral:

\begin{align} &= \left [ \frac{1}{54} (3(1)+1 - 4 \ln 3(1)+1) \right] - \left [ \frac{1}{54} (3(0)+1 - 4 \ln 3(0)+1) \right] \\ &= 0.0234289431 \end{align}

Finally, we can add these numbers to get our $p(\text{mistake})$: $$p(\text{mistake}) = 0.04814680398 + 0.0234289431 = 0.07157574708$$ Or, about a $7\%$ misclassification error rate. We also seem to be accepting more false

Overall, I'd say the z-score seems pretty useful for determining the validity of a claim, if we can use it to get only a 7% chance of making a mistake. We can also see that we have a higher chance of a false negative (turning away a valid claim, ~4.8%), than a false negative (accepting a false claim, ~2.3%). Depending on the insurance company's utility/loss matrix, this may or may not make sense.
